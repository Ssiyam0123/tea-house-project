What is Matcha? History and culture

Origin & History

During the Tang and Song Dynasties in China, powdered tea was popular among the aristocracy and Buddhist monks. In Chinese monasteries, tea leaves were ground into a fine powder and whisked in bowls, serving as both a medicine and a stimulant during long hours of meditation.

In 1191, the Buddhist monk Eisai traveled to China to study Zen. Upon his return to Japan, he brought back tea seeds and the powdered preparation method. Eisai planted the seeds at a temple in Kyoto and wrote the first Japanese book on tea, highlighting its spiritual and physical health benefits.

Production Technology

The traditional, step-by-step craftsmanship of premium matcha.

01

Shading from the Sun

About 20 days before harvest, the tea bushes are shaded under bamboo mats or nets. This reduces sunlight, boosting chlorophyll levels to turn the leaves a vibrant jade green and increasing the L-theanine amino acid content.

02

Traditional Hand-picking

Only the youngest, most tender tea leaves (called Tencha) are carefully hand-picked in early spring. This ensures that the matcha has a sweet, smooth flavor profile free of bitter stems.

03

Steaming and Air-Drying

Immediately after harvest, the leaves are steamed to stop oxidation and preserve their natural green color and health benefits. They are then gently dried in large chambers without crushing them.

04

Slow Stone Grinding

After removing stems and veins, the pure leaves are slowly ground using traditional granite stone mills. It takes about an hour to grind just 30 grams of matcha to prevent friction heat from damaging the flavor.

How to distinguish high-quality Matcha?

Spot the difference between authentic ceremonial Matcha and regular green tea powder.

Authentic Matcha

Ceremonial Grade Matcha

  • Vibrant jade green color (due to high chlorophyll).
  • Extremely fine talcum-powder texture.
  • Naturally sweet, rich umami taste with no bitterness.
  • Ground slowly using granite stone mills.
Regular Tea Powder

Fumatsucha / Green Tea Powder

  • Dull, brownish or yellowish green color.
  • Coarser texture, leaves grittiness in the cup.
  • Bitter, astringent taste requiring sugar or milk.
  • Ground quickly using industrial metal blades.
